The Chicago Affair

when mainstream institutionsclaimed the bible’s authority for themselves

by Thompson Fletcher

(Excerpt from an as yet unpublished work.)

“I mean, the Bible is just a book written by men, right?”

And then copied over and over, thousands of times, by scribes, who were all human! And humans are imperfect! We make errors! I’m sure you might be able to find some in this book you’re holding right now. And not only did absolute truck loads of scribal errors make their way into the copying of the text over centuries, and centuries, and centuries, but then, after all that error-laden, mistake ridden sloppy copying, like a centuries long game of drunken ‘telephone’, those texts were then translated by human interpreters! Didn’t they almost certainly make errors in their translation? Weren’t they arrogant and biased, with judgement clouded by their own lust for worldly power over others? Or, hopelessly restricted by the commands of a tyrannical King? And how could we ever be sure of what the text is really supposed to say?

The above paragraph is the author’s attempt to capture (with a little bit of exaggeration for dramatic effect) the view point that is held by most of the civilized world about the Bible. And they have good reason to hold it: it’s what the big Bible colleges are teaching! All the best paid, most expert textual scholars all agree, from Bruce Metzger to Bart Ehrman, from Norman Geisler to Frank Turek, from David Burggraff to Michael Heiser, from John Gerstner to R. C. Sproul, from Jack Macarthur to John Macarthur, just about every body in the field of textual scholarship, and by extension, mainstream evangelism, unanimously affirms that the biblical text has gone through gobs and gobs of error prone transmission since the ‘‘original autographs’’ were written down. And by original autographs, we of course mean the pages and parchments that the original biblical writers ‘‘must have’’ penned with their own ostensibly very inky hands, from Moses and the Prophets in the Old Testament to Matthew, Mark, Luke, John, Peter, Paul, James and Jude in the New. And this process of transmission has been affected by these alleged ‘‘transmission errors’’ so much so that divine inspiration can only be attributed to these original autographs — these so far elusive, inspired pieces of paper which, by what is seen in the professional field of biblical scholarship as a small matter of inconvenience, no one seems to have.

And this is not just the author’s best guess about the professional field of textual criticism’s unanimously affirmed position regarding textual transmission — or in more simple terms, ‘‘Bible copying’’. The position that only the so-called original autographs are inspired can easily be found signed, sealed and delivered in the statements of faith and doctrinal proclamations across the official web sites of virtually all of the largest Bible colleges and institutions. The very same institutions wherein many millions of well intentioned Christians go, and send their kids, to learn about the origin, history, transmission and translation of the words of Almighty God. Yes, you read that right. These ‘‘professors’’ of the faith do not believe that anyone, anywhere has the inspired words of God, and they see this as an inconvenience, but not a problem.

Before we get to why they don’t see it as a problem (hint: it’s because they are selling the ‘‘solution’’) let me show you some of these doctrinal proclamations so you can see them for yourself first hand.

Let’s start with the Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy1. We can skip right on down to Article X, where the meat of the issue lies. (Fun fact: the letter capital ‘X’ does not appear in the text of the Bible! On the contrary, in modern culture it is well associated with sinful activities!)

Here is article X in it’s entirety (emphasis mine):

Article X.

We affirm that inspiration, strictly speaking, applies only to the autographic text of Scripture, which in the providence of God can be ascertained from available manuscripts with great accuracy. We further affirm that copies and translations of Scripture are the Word of God to the extent that they faithfully represent the original.

We deny that any essential element of the Christian faith is affected by the absence of the autographs. We further deny that this absence renders the assertion of Biblical inerrancy invalid or irrelevant.

Page 5, Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy.

(Sounds like somebody might be in denial.)

And here’s a little old Bible verse to compare that bit of supposedly faithful and Biblical thinking with:

Heaven and earth shall pass away, but my words shall not pass away.2

- Jesus Christ, Matthew 24:35; Luke 21:33; Mark 13:31.

Boy, is that a whopper! Let’s try to break it down. First, the statement claims that only the original autographs are inspired. Wow. So if I don’t have the original autographs, I don’t know what the Word of God says. Big problem! But no worries, the statement goes on to address it, by saying that the autographic text of Scripture can somehow be, and I quote directly, ‘‘ascertained’’, and so with, I quote again, ‘‘great accuracy’’. But notice, not ‘‘perfect’’ accuracy. Just ‘‘great’’ accuracy. Well immediately I have a problem with that, and you should too. And by the way, every unbeliever, every self titled atheist, agnostic, doubter, and skeptic understands this problem very well.

The problem is this: How do I know which parts have been properly ‘‘ascertained’’ (that’s a funny word by the way. You know what they say about ascertaining: the one thing it makes certain, is that you’ll certainly end up looking like an ass! Alright, enough donkeying around.) Surely you see the problem, don’t you? If it has to be ascertained, and I can never know which parts have been ‘‘greatly’’ ascertained, and which parts can’t be ascertained at all, and which parts have been somewhat kind of sort of mostly ascertained, how do I know which parts have been certainly ascertained, and which parts have just been cobbled together by the scholars as a best guess? In practical terms, how do I know which words in the Bible I can trust?

This lack of consistency has some serious implications! Because if I don’t know which parts I can trust, I can’t trust any of it! Because I’ll never know if they got the part I’m believing in right or not!

And this is not just some weirdo conspiracy theory interpretation of the Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy. It is not a some lofty, hypothetical proposition that some people may eventually someday think this way as a result of the doctrinal position embodied by this statement.

This is the mainstream position of unbelievers and Christians alike, regarding the Bible and whether it can be relied on or not.

Unbelievers deny the Bible because it’s just a book cobbled together by, as one woman put it yesterday during a door to door outreach effort, ‘‘a bunch of white men’’. And this is an wholly accurate criticism, at least according to the Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy. A bunch of white men signed that statement and say that they and their colleagues in the textual criticism field, are the anointed ones who can correctly ‘‘ascertain’’ the text of the ‘‘original autographs’’, and the rest of us just better trust and listen to them.

And if you don’t believe me, or if you think I’m stretching it a bit, just look at the way they worded it: ‘‘We affirm,’’ and ‘‘We deny,’’ as if their affirmation or denial has any bearing on the truth whatsoever. Romans chapter three has something to say about that, in regard to keeping and believing the word of God:

... unto them were committed the oracles of God. For what if some did not believe? shall their unbelief make the faith of God without effect? God forbid: yea, let God be true, but every man a liar;2

- Romans 3:2-4.

Let’s see if the Chicago Statement of Errancy (forgive me, I renamed it) has anything else to say about the transmission and translation of the Bible. Say, in it’s so-titled section (emphasis mine).

Transmission and Translation

Since God has nowhere promised an inerrant transmission of Scripture, it is necessary to affirm that only the autographic text of the original documents was inspired and to maintain the need of textual criticism as a means of detecting any slips that may have crept into the text in the course of its transmission. The verdict of this science, however, is that the Hebrew and Greek text appear to be amazingly well preserved, so that we are amply justified in affirming, with the Westminster Confession, a singular providence of God in this matter and in declaring that the authority of Scripture is in no way jeopardized by the fact that the copies we possess are not entirely error-free.

Similarly, no translation, is or can be perfect, and all translations are an additional step away from the autographa. Yet the verdict of linguistic science is that English-speaking Christians, at least, are exceedingly well served in these days with a host of excellent translations and have no cause for hesitating to conclude that the true Word of God is within their reach. Indeed, in view of the frequent repetition in Scripture of the main matters with which it deals and also of the Holy Spirit’s constant witness to and through the Word, no serious translation of Holy Scripture will so destroy its meaning as to render it unable to make its reader ‘‘wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus’’ 2 Tim. 3:15.

Page 10, Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy.

Boy, that’s a doozy! So many twistings of logic, posturing words, slippery statements, and flowery, academic-sounding, pontificatory blabberings, you’d almost not even notice that it’s chock full of anti-biblical reasoning, outright deception, illogic, and just a whole lot of self congratulatory snootiness. Where to begin? Well, why don’t we start at the top!

‘‘Since God has nowhere promised an inerrant transmission of Scripture...’’ Well hold it right there folks. This might be the end of the show already. We’ve already looked at Matthew 24:35, where Jesus Christ Himself promises precisely that. ‘‘My words shall not pass away.’’ That sounds a whole lot like a promise that his words will, well, not pass way: in other words, they will be preserved, or to use the slippery language of the modern scholars, ‘‘transmitted’’, perfectly, or ‘‘inerrantly’’, from one century to the next, even beyond the end of Heaven and earth. That’s a promise right from the mouth of Jesus Christ that his words will be preserved, not lost, not errantly transmitted, but preserved down to the very word, for ever. But this is not the end of the matter. The very next verse of scripture after the one cited by the Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy, at the end of their section on Transmission and Translation, says this:

All scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness: that the man of God may be made perfect, throughly furnished unto all good works.2

- 1 Timothy 3:16-17.

Did you catch that? Right in the beginning of the verse: ‘‘All scripture.’’ Not some of it. Not the just the red letters that represent the words of Jesus Christ. Not just this chapter or that chapter, this book or that book of the Bible. Not a cherry picked selection of verses ‘‘ascertained’’ by textual scholars who deny the authority of later manuscripts. ‘‘All scripture’’.

‘‘...is given by inspiration of God...’’

θεόπνευστος

The Koine Greek word used at ‘‘inspiration of God’’, shown above, can be transliterated as ‘‘theopneustos’’, a combination of the word ‘theos’ which translates roughly as ‘God’, and ‘pneuma’ which can be rendered as ‘breath’ or ‘spirit’. So the Bible says that God inspired, or breathed, every word of the Holy scriptures. And some simple logic says that if Jesus really is God, as he claims to be in many places of scripture, and according to Jesus, his own words will not pass away, as he promises in his famous discourse with his disciples on the Mount of Olives, then neither shall any of the holy scriptures pass away. According to 2 Timothy 3:16, they too are His words, breathed by Him. And so they too shall not pass away.

For the testimony of Jesus is the spirit of prophecy.2

- Revelation 19:10.

And thus, we find in the Bible that in fact, there is a strong scriptural promise regarding textual transmission, to use the scholars’ own slippery terms again. It is by all measures an extremely strong promise. Not a single word of scripture will pass away. That would mean that every word would rightly survive centuries and even millennia of transmission, that all of heaven and earth shall be lost before one single word of scripture is lost. That’s a very strong promise indeed. Here’s another one:

For verily I say unto you, Till heaven and earth pass, one jot or one tittle shall in no wise pass from the law, till all be fulfilled.2

- Jesus Christ, Matthew 5:18.

If one is a Christian, and believes that Jesus Christ is God, then Matthew 5:18 is yet another crystal clear promise from the very mouth of God to preserve not only every word, but every letter of the law, which in the context given, plainly refers to the holy scriptures.

Yes, folks. If you believe that Jesus is God, then God did promise to preserve His words inerrantly in Matthew 5:18.

And we’ll find out in a moment why the latter is true for these men.

In the words of Jesus Christ: ‘‘Ye hypocrites, well did Esaias prophesy of you, saying, This people draweth nigh unto me with their mouth, and honoureth me with their lips; but their heart is far from me.’’

Between fools or knaves, I don’t know which option is right, but I can tell you that I don’t trust their teaching, their reasoning, their weaseling words, for one more jot or one more tittle after being privy to a whopper that gigantic - ‘‘Since nowhere has God promised...’’ — right out of the gates.

But let’s keep going with their statement, because it gives us a great window into the current state of affairs in the big time professional field of biblical scholarship and modern day evangelism. A lot of big names have signed the Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy, and echoed it in their own doctrinal statements on the inspiration of scripture.

Since they have denied God’s many promises to preserve his words unto and beyond the ends of both heaven and earth, they now lean on these supposedly autographic pieces of paper as the only authoritative Scriptures. So when they say in their statement ‘‘we believe the Holy Scriptures’’ and ‘‘God inspired the Scriptures,’’ and all these sorts of affirmations and denials that they’ve padded out the rest of the document with, what they really are talking about are these mysterious pieces of paper supposedly written by the very hand of each Prophet and Apostle.

So in essence, claiming that these pieces of paper have the authority of God’s divine authorship behind them is essentially meaningless, though the statement vehemently ‘‘denies’’ it’s meaninglessness. It’s been my experience that most liars, at least in the beginning, vehemently deny that they are liars. Their next step often is to deny that their lies made any difference. Hmm. ‘‘...and to maintain the need of textual criticism as a means of detecting any slips that may have crept into the text in the course of its transmission.’’ Ah. So of course, they don’t want you to throw the unequivocal authority of the original manuscripts out with the bath water of their non existence. They simply want you to ascribe that authority, the ultimate authority of God’s word, on to those responsible for this thing called ‘‘textual criticism,’’ in other words: themselves.

Let’s see how that turns out in practice! How trustworthy are these textual scholars, who have appointed themselves responsible for deciding what God really said, and what He didn’t? Maybe we ought to look at some of the fruits of their labor: their modern translations of the Bible!

So far, we’ve looked at the New Testament. Let’s take a look in the old for a moment. Here’s a promise in the Old Testament that God will preserve his words, inerrantly:

The words of the LORD are pure words: as silver tried in a furnace of earth, purified seven times. Thou shalt keep them, O LORD, thou shalt preserve them from this generation for ever.2

- Psalm 12:6-7.

Would you be surprised at this point to learn that nearly all modern versions of the Bible render this verse differently? For instance, the ESV says thus for verse 7: ‘‘You, O LORD, will keep them; you will guard us from this generation forever.’’4 Guard ‘us’? Who is the ‘us’? The ESV translators, no doubt, foresaw that some may have this question. There is an annotation on the word ‘us’. It reads: Or ‘guard him’.

The NASB puts ‘him’ right into the verse: ‘‘You, Lord, will keep them; You will protect him from this generation forever.’’5

‘Him’. Hmm. Ok, so they are tethering the verb only to an antecedent back two verses prior (and folks, make no mistake: this is what we in the business might call, a stretch) where there is no risk of implying that the promise of inerrant transmission of God’s words exists. They’ve simply shifted the object to only implying the inerrant transmission of the poor and the needy, which are mentioned in verse 5. As a friend of mine, Dr. David Brown, once put it: ‘‘If God cannot preserve His word, how is He expected to preserve His people?’’

To be fair, the Authorized Version also places a note at this spot, pointing out the same literal rendering ‘him’. But it expounds on the application of such a rendering in Hebrew, exemplifying that when applied to a plural object, the singular verb implies an ‘‘every one of them’’ sense of usage. And in this context, there is intentional ambiguity. In other words, the verb can be applied to both the words, and those who keep them. The King James Bible preserves this. The modern Bibles take it out. I wonder what their motivation was? To soften the doctrine of providential preservation? To hide the promise of God to preserve his words for ever? Couldn’t be! Either way, the chopping of the antecedent in modern versions certainly takes some of the punch out of that verse! Here is a verse in the Bible about just such a thing:

Thus saith the LORD; Stand in the court of the LORD’S house, and speak unto all the cities of Judah, which come to worship in the LORD’S house, all the words that I command thee to speak unto them; diminish not a word:2

- Jeremiah 26:2.

Would you be surprised to learn that modern versions alter that very word ‘‘diminish’’, weakening and diminishing its meaning?

NASB: Do not omit a word!5

NIV: do not omit a word.3

ESV: do not hold back a word.4

Ah, so in these versions, it’s okay if I diminish a word, like for example, removing the promise of scriptural preservation by using pronoun choice to limit the scope of the verb in Psalm 12:7. As long as I just don’t take out the word entirely, we’re all good!
